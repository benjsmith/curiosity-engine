### Title: Learning Transferable Visual Models From Natural Language Supervision (CLIP)

### Authors: Alec Radford, Jong Wook Kim, Chris Hallacy, Aditya Ramesh, Gabriel Goh, Sandhini Agarwal, Girish Sastry, Amanda Askell, Pamela Mishkin, Jack Clark, Gretchen Krueger, Ilya Sutskever (2021)

### arXiv id: 2103.00020

### Abstract and Introduction

State-of-the-art computer vision systems at the time of the paper were trained on fixed, crowd-labeled datasets such as ImageNet with a predefined set of object categories. This paradigm limits models in two ways: (i) acquiring additional labeled data is expensive, and (ii) the resulting classifier is only able to predict the classes it was trained on, so zero-shot transfer to new categories typically requires fine-tuning on new labeled data. By contrast, natural language is a vastly richer and cheaper source of supervision: the internet contains billions of image-text pairs where captions, alt-text, and surrounding text implicitly describe the visual content.

CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training) scales up this idea. The authors curate a dataset of 400 million image-text pairs from the public internet and train a joint image-text model with a simple contrastive objective: given a batch of N image-text pairs, predict which of the N x N possible pairings actually occurred. The resulting model learns a shared embedding space where matched images and captions have high cosine similarity, and mismatched ones have low similarity. At inference time, CLIP performs zero-shot classification by embedding each class name as a text prompt (e.g., "a photo of a {class}") and predicting the class whose text embedding has highest cosine similarity with the image embedding.

This allows a single pretrained model to be evaluated on arbitrary visual classification tasks without any task-specific fine-tuning. CLIP is benchmarked on over 30 different computer vision datasets covering OCR, action recognition in videos, geo-localization, fine-grained object classification, and more. It matches the accuracy of the original ResNet-50 on ImageNet zero-shot, without using any of the 1.28M ImageNet training examples. On many datasets, it rivals fully supervised baselines. The authors also study robustness to distribution shift: CLIP's zero-shot classifier is dramatically more robust on ImageNet-V2, ImageNet-A, ImageNet-R, and ImageNet-Sketch than any supervised ImageNet model.

The introduction situates CLIP in a line of work that reached back through VirTex, ConVIRT, and ICMLM. Prior natural-language-supervised vision models had struggled to match supervised ImageNet baselines, primarily because they were trained on small datasets (COCO, YFCC). CLIP's key empirical finding is that with enough data (400M pairs) and a simple contrastive objective, the approach scales and surpasses supervised baselines on transfer.

### Methods

Dataset construction (WIT). The authors construct a new dataset called WIT (WebImageText) with 400 million image-text pairs collected by searching the public internet for image-text pairs matching any of 500,000 queries. Queries were constructed from English Wikipedia words with counts above 100, augmented with bi-grams and WordNet synsets. The dataset is balanced by capping the number of pairs per query at 20,000, which improves sample efficiency. WIT is comparable in total word count to the WebText dataset used to train GPT-2.

Contrastive objective. Given a mini-batch of N image-text pairs (I_i, T_i), CLIP trains an image encoder f_I and a text encoder f_T to maximize the cosine similarity of the N matched pairs while minimizing the similarity of the N^2 - N unmatched pairs. Image embeddings u_i = f_I(I_i) and text embeddings v_j = f_T(T_j) are L2-normalized. The logits are s_{ij} = (u_i . v_j) / tau, where tau is a learned temperature parameter. The loss is the symmetric cross-entropy over the image-to-text and text-to-image similarity matrices (InfoNCE-style).

The authors note that they initially tried a generative caption-prediction objective (predict the exact caption from the image) and found it was 3-4x less sample-efficient than the contrastive objective. They also tried a bag-of-words prediction objective, which was better than captioning but still 3x less efficient than contrastive.

Architecture. CLIP trains a family of models. The image encoder is either a ResNet (ResNet-50, ResNet-101, ResNet-50x4, ResNet-50x16, ResNet-50x64 following EfficientNet-style scaling) or a Vision Transformer (ViT-B/32, ViT-B/16, ViT-L/14). The ResNets use antialiased blurpool and replace the global average pooling with attention pooling. The ViTs follow Dosovitskiy et al. (2020). A final linear projection maps the encoder output to the joint embedding space of dimension 512-1024.

The text encoder is a 63M-parameter 12-layer 512-wide Transformer with 8 heads, applied to a BPE tokenization with 49,152 vocab size. Maximum text length is 76 tokens. The activation of the [EOS] token at the last layer is taken as the text representation and projected to the joint embedding space. The text encoder is fixed in size across image encoder scales; the authors found that scaling the text encoder had smaller effects than scaling the image encoder.

Training. Models are trained for 32 epochs over WIT with very large batch sizes (32,768). The Adam optimizer with decoupled weight decay is used with cosine decay and a 10,000-step linear warmup. Mixed-precision training is used, and the contrastive loss is computed across all GPUs via an all-gather to get effective batch sizes up to 32,768. The temperature tau is parameterized in log space and learned, clipped to prevent logits exceeding 100. The largest models (ResNet-50x64, ViT-L/14) take 12 days on 592 V100s and 18 days on 256 V100s respectively.

Zero-shot classification. To evaluate zero-shot transfer, each class label is embedded as one or more text prompts (e.g., "a photo of a {label}", "a photo of a big {label}"). Multiple prompts are averaged to form a class-specific text embedding; these embeddings act as a classifier weight matrix. At inference, an image is embedded and matched against the class embeddings by cosine similarity. For some datasets, prompt engineering and ensembling over 80 prompts improves accuracy by roughly 5 points.

Linear probe evaluation. The authors also evaluate the pretrained image encoder by training a logistic regression classifier on its frozen features, a standard transfer-learning protocol. This complements zero-shot evaluation by measuring the quality of the learned representation independent of the text-side prompts.

### Key Results

Zero-shot ImageNet: ViT-L/14 at 336x336 resolution reaches 76.2% top-1 on ImageNet zero-shot, matching the original ResNet-50 (76.1%) trained on the full ImageNet training set. With prompt ensembling, the top-1 rises to 76.2%. At 224x224, the zero-shot top-1 is 75.3%. Smaller CLIP models: ResNet-50 CLIP achieves 59.6% zero-shot; ViT-B/32 achieves 63.2%.

Transfer across 27 datasets: CLIP's zero-shot classifier outperforms a linear probe on ResNet-50 features (trained on ImageNet) on 16 of 27 datasets, including STL-10 (99.3% zero-shot), Oxford Pets, Stanford Cars, and Country211. It underperforms on specialized tasks such as EuroSAT, PatchCamelyon, and CLEVR counting, where natural-language descriptions poorly match the class concepts.

Linear-probe evaluation: CLIP ViT-L/14 linear probe achieves state-of-the-art on 21 of 27 datasets relative to prior self-supervised and supervised baselines. Average accuracy is 84.5%, versus 79.0% for a ResNet-50x4 trained with SimCLRv2, and 73.5% for a supervised ResNet-50.

Robustness under distribution shift: zero-shot CLIP is far more robust than supervised ImageNet models to natural distribution shift. On ImageNet-V2, CLIP ViT-L/14 retains 70.1% top-1 (vs 76.2% on ImageNet, a 6.1 point drop) where a supervised ResNet-101 drops from 76.2% to 64.3% (11.9 point drop). On ImageNet-Sketch, CLIP reaches 60.2% vs 24.1% for ResNet-101. On ImageNet-A, CLIP reaches 77.2% vs 2.7% for ResNet-101. On ImageNet-R, CLIP reaches 88.9% vs 37.7%. The authors introduce the concept of "effective robustness" (robustness beyond what is predicted by in-distribution accuracy) and show CLIP has far higher effective robustness than all prior supervised baselines.

OCR and other specialized capabilities: CLIP exhibits surprising emergent behaviors such as transcribing text in images (SST-2 via text rendered as images: 67.9% zero-shot), recognizing famous people, and locating geographic scenes (Country211: 31.3% top-1 zero-shot).

### Conclusion

CLIP demonstrates that natural language supervision, applied at sufficient scale, yields visual representations that transfer broadly and robustly to downstream tasks without any task-specific fine-tuning. By training a simple contrastive image-text objective on 400M web-collected pairs, the largest CLIP models match or exceed fully supervised baselines across dozens of benchmarks and show dramatically improved robustness to distribution shift. The paper also highlights limitations: CLIP still underperforms on fine-grained and abstract tasks, is sensitive to prompt wording, and inherits biases from the web-scraped dataset. Nonetheless, the CLIP recipe has proven foundational for subsequent multimodal systems including DALL-E 2, Stable Diffusion, Flamingo, and a broad class of open-vocabulary detection and segmentation models. The central lesson is that linking vision and language through a shared embedding space unlocks transfer capabilities that supervised single-modality training does not.
