### Title: Mixtral of Experts

### Authors: Albert Q. Jiang, Alexandre Sablayrolles, Antoine Roux, Arthur Mensch, Blanche Savary, Chris Bamford, Devendra Singh Chaplot, Diego de las Casas, Emma Bou Hanna, Florian Bressand, Gianna Lengyel, Guillaume Bour, Guillaume Lample, Lelio Renard Lavaud, Lucile Saulnier, Marie-Anne Lachaux, Pierre Stock, Sandeep Subramanian, Sophia Yang, Szymon Antoniak, Teven Le Scao, Theophile Gervet, Thibaut Lavril, Thomas Wang, Timothee Lacroix, William El Sayed (2024)

### arXiv id: 2401.04088

### Abstract and Introduction

Mixtral 8x7B is a Sparse Mixture of Experts (SMoE) language model introduced by Mistral AI. It has the same architecture as Mistral 7B, with the major distinction that each feedforward layer is replaced by a block of 8 feedforward experts. At each layer, for every token, a router network selects two experts to process the current hidden state and combines their outputs as a weighted sum. Although each token sees only two experts at a time, the selected experts can differ at each timestep. As a result, each token has access to 47B total parameters but only uses roughly 13B active parameters during inference. This design allows Mixtral to increase model capacity while maintaining controlled compute cost and latency: the model processes tokens at roughly the speed and cost of a 13B dense model, while carrying the representational power of a much larger model.

Mixtral was trained with a 32k token context window using multilingual data. It outperforms or matches the much larger Llama 2 70B and GPT-3.5 on most standard benchmarks, in particular on mathematics, code generation, and multilingual tasks. The paper also introduces Mixtral 8x7B - Instruct, a model fine-tuned to follow instructions using supervised fine-tuning (SFT) followed by Direct Preference Optimization (DPO). Mixtral - Instruct surpasses GPT-3.5 Turbo, Claude-2.1, Gemini Pro, and Llama 2 70B - chat on several human evaluation benchmarks. Both the base and the instruction-tuned models are released under the Apache 2.0 license, freely available for commercial and research use.

The introduction motivates sparse mixture-of-experts models as a promising pathway to scale model capacity efficiently. Dense transformers require the FLOPs cost to grow proportionally with parameter count; conversely, MoE architectures decouple total parameters from per-token compute. While mixture-of-experts has a long history dating to Jacobs et al. (1991) and Shazeer et al. (2017)'s sparsely-gated MoE layers, bringing MoE into the general-purpose instruction-following regime with strong multilingual and reasoning capabilities had remained challenging. Mixtral demonstrates that carefully designed MoE models can match or exceed much larger dense counterparts across a wide distribution of tasks while remaining practical to serve.

### Methods

Architecture. Mixtral is based on a transformer architecture very close to Mistral 7B, with key modifications for sparse routing. The model has 32 layers, dimensionality d_model = 4096, 32 attention heads with 8 key-value heads (grouped-query attention), a head dimension of 128, vocabulary size of 32000, and a hidden feedforward dimension of 14336. Attention uses sliding window attention and rotary position embeddings (RoPE) with base frequency theta = 1000000, enabling a 32k context length.

The key innovation is replacing each feedforward block with a Mixture-of-Experts layer. Each MoE layer contains n = 8 expert feedforward networks, each structurally identical to the Mistral 7B FFN (SwiGLU with hidden dim 14336). For each input token x, a gating network computes logits via a linear projection W_g, selects the top-K logits, and applies softmax only over those. Formally, with K = 2:

G(x) = Softmax(TopK(x . W_g))
y = sum over i in TopK of G(x)_i * E_i(x)

where E_i is the i-th expert. Thus only K = 2 of 8 experts are evaluated per token. With 2 of 8 experts active, Mixtral has 46.7B total parameters but approximately 12.9B active parameters per token. The router is small: an additive overhead of 8 x 4096 parameters per layer.

Training. Mixtral was pretrained with a 32k context from scratch using multilingual data covering English, French, German, Italian, and Spanish, along with code. The paper does not disclose the exact pretraining corpus size or token count, but notes the model was trained to perform well on both monolingual English benchmarks and multilingual evaluation, with explicit emphasis on mathematics and code generation. Training uses standard causal language modeling loss.

Inference and Serving. While Mixtral's total parameter count (47B) is large, only 13B are activated per token, so inference FLOPs are roughly equivalent to a 13B dense model. However, all 47B parameters must reside in memory (or be paged), making memory bandwidth a practical bottleneck. The paper discusses expert parallelism and model parallelism strategies for efficient serving, and notes that inference on Mixtral can be accelerated using techniques such as MegaBlocks-style block-sparse kernels.

Instruction Fine-Tuning. Mixtral 8x7B - Instruct is produced via supervised fine-tuning on an instruction dataset followed by Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) on a paired preference dataset. The authors do not release the fine-tuning data but describe the procedure as a standard alignment pipeline.

Routing Analysis. The paper examines the behavior of the learned router across layers, domains, and token positions. The authors find that expert assignment does not show strong topical specialization (e.g., an expert for "biology"), but rather exhibits syntactic patterns: the router tends to route consecutive tokens to the same expert and shows some structure based on token identity. The distribution of expert usage is approximately uniform across the training set, suggesting healthy load balance. Specifically, when the authors look at layers 0, 15, and 31 on The Pile, expert assignment varies only modestly across domains like arXiv, GitHub, PubMed, and StackExchange, reinforcing the view that routing learned syntactic rather than semantic specialization.

### Key Results

On standard English benchmarks, Mixtral 8x7B outperforms or matches Llama 2 70B despite using a fraction of active parameters. On MMLU (5-shot), Mixtral achieves 70.6% vs 69.9% for Llama 2 70B and 70.0% for GPT-3.5. On HellaSwag (10-shot), Mixtral scores 84.4% vs 85.4% for Llama 2 70B. On ARC Challenge (25-shot), Mixtral reaches 66.0% vs 54.9% for Llama 2 70B. On WinoGrande (5-shot), Mixtral achieves 77.2%.

Mixtral especially shines on code and math. On GSM8K (5-shot maj@8 where applicable), Mixtral achieves 74.4% compared to Llama 2 70B's 63.2%. On MATH (4-shot), Mixtral reaches 28.4% vs 13.8% for Llama 2 70B. On HumanEval (0-shot pass@1), Mixtral scores 40.2% vs 32.2% for Llama 2 70B. On MBPP (3-shot), Mixtral achieves 60.7%.

For multilingual benchmarks, Mixtral significantly outperforms Llama 2 70B on French, German, Italian, and Spanish MMLU and ARC. For example, on French MMLU, Mixtral reaches 62.5% vs 45.0% for Llama 2 70B. Long-context retrieval experiments show near-perfect passkey retrieval up to the full 32k context regardless of where the passkey is inserted.

The instruction-tuned Mixtral 8x7B - Instruct reaches an MT-Bench score of 8.30, surpassing Llama 2 70B - chat (6.86) and performing competitively with GPT-3.5 Turbo, Claude-2.1, and Gemini Pro. On LMSYS Chatbot Arena, it ranks above all open models at release time. Bias evaluations on BBQ and BOLD show Mixtral exhibits less bias than Llama 2 70B on several demographic axes.

### Conclusion

Mixtral 8x7B demonstrates that sparse mixture-of-experts architectures offer a favorable compute-capability frontier for large language models. By activating only 13B of 47B parameters per token, the model achieves inference cost comparable to a 13B dense model while matching or exceeding the quality of a 70B dense model across most evaluations, with particularly strong gains in mathematics, code, and multilingual tasks. The instruction-tuned variant is competitive with the strongest proprietary chat models of its generation. By releasing both variants under Apache 2.0, Mixtral substantially advances the state of openly available language models. Open questions raised include the lack of clear topical specialization among experts, strategies for more efficient MoE inference on commodity hardware, and scaling MoE models to even larger total-parameter regimes while preserving routing quality and training stability.
