# BERT: Pre-training of Deep Bidirectional Transformers for Language Understanding

**Authors:** Jacob Devlin, Ming-Wei Chang, Kenton Lee, Kristina Toutanova (2018)
**arXiv:** 1810.04805

## Abstract

This paper introduces a new language representation model called BERT, which stands for Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers. Unlike recent language representation models such as ELMo and the OpenAI GPT, BERT is designed to pre-train deep bidirectional representations from unlabeled text by jointly conditioning on both left and right context in all layers. As a result, the pre-trained BERT model can be fine-tuned with just one additional output layer to create state-of-the-art models for a wide range of tasks, such as question answering and language inference, without substantial task-specific architectural modifications. BERT is conceptually simple and empirically powerful. It obtains new state-of-the-art results on eleven natural language processing tasks, including pushing the GLUE score to 80.5% (a 7.7% point absolute improvement), MultiNLI accuracy to 86.7% (a 4.6% absolute improvement), SQuAD v1.1 question answering test F1 to 93.2 (a 1.5 point absolute improvement), and SQuAD v2.0 test F1 to 83.1 (a 5.1 point absolute improvement). The paper argues that unidirectional constraints on previous language models limit the power of the pre-trained representations, especially for sentence-level and token-level tasks where context from both directions is important. To alleviate this constraint, BERT introduces a masked language model (MLM) objective inspired by the Cloze task, along with a next sentence prediction (NSP) objective. The simplicity of fine-tuning, combined with the strength of the underlying representations, made BERT a dominant approach for language understanding tasks for years after publication.

## Methods

BERT uses a multi-layer bidirectional Transformer encoder based on the original implementation of Vaswani et al. 2017. Two model sizes are primarily reported. BERT_BASE has L = 12 transformer layers, H = 768 hidden size, A = 12 self-attention heads, and a total of 110 million parameters. BERT_LARGE has L = 24 layers, H = 1024 hidden size, A = 16 heads, and 340 million parameters. The input representation is constructed by summing token embeddings, segment embeddings, and learned positional embeddings. A special [CLS] token is prepended to every input and its final hidden state is used for classification tasks; a [SEP] token separates sentence pairs.

Two pre-training objectives are used. The first is a masked language model (MLM): 15% of the WordPiece tokens in each sequence are selected uniformly at random, and of those, 80% are replaced with a special [MASK] token, 10% are replaced with a random token, and 10% are left unchanged. The model must predict the original identity of the selected tokens using cross-entropy loss. Because the [MASK] token does not appear at fine-tuning time, the mixture of replacement strategies reduces the train/test mismatch. This bidirectional objective distinguishes BERT from left-to-right language models such as GPT.

The second objective is next sentence prediction (NSP), in which two sentences A and B are concatenated and the model is trained to predict whether B is the actual next sentence of A in the corpus (positive example) or a random sentence from the corpus (negative example). NSP targets pairwise sentence tasks such as natural language inference and question answering. Later work has questioned its necessity, but in the original paper it is shown to contribute meaningfully to downstream performance.

Pre-training is performed on the BooksCorpus (800M words) and English Wikipedia (2,500M words), using only the text passages and ignoring lists, tables, and headers, for a total of roughly 3.3 billion words. Training runs for 1M steps with batch size 256 sequences of length up to 512, using Adam with learning rate 1e-4, beta_1 = 0.9, beta_2 = 0.999, L2 weight decay of 0.01, learning rate warmup over the first 10,000 steps, and linear decay. A gelu activation function and a dropout probability of 0.1 are used on all layers.

For fine-tuning, task-specific input and output layers are added and all parameters are updated end-to-end. Typically a small number of epochs (2 to 4) with batch sizes of 16 or 32 and learning rates in the range 2e-5 to 5e-5 are sufficient. BERT also supports token-level tasks such as named entity recognition and span extraction for question answering, by using the final hidden states of individual input tokens.

## Key Results

BERT achieves state-of-the-art performance on 11 NLP tasks at the time of publication. On the GLUE benchmark, BERT_LARGE achieves an average score of 80.5, compared to 75.1 for OpenAI GPT and 70.0 for a BiLSTM with ELMo, representing a 7.7 point absolute improvement. It improves over prior state-of-the-art on MNLI (86.7% accuracy), QQP, QNLI, SST-2, CoLA, and other GLUE subtasks.

On SQuAD v1.1, BERT_LARGE with an ensemble and the TriviaQA augmentation achieves a test F1 of 93.2 and an EM of 87.4, a 1.5 F1 improvement over the previous best. Even BERT_LARGE single model achieves 91.8 F1. On SQuAD v2.0, which includes unanswerable questions, BERT_LARGE achieves a test F1 of 83.1 and EM of 80.0, a 5.1 F1 point improvement over prior work.

On SWAG, a grounded commonsense inference task, BERT_LARGE achieves 86.3% accuracy, outperforming the best prior baseline by 27.1 points and exceeding human performance reported at the time. Ablations show that removing the next sentence prediction objective hurts performance on QNLI, MNLI, and SQuAD. A left-to-right-only model (analogous to OpenAI GPT but trained on identical data) underperforms BERT by a wide margin on all tasks. Scaling experiments demonstrate consistent gains from larger models, even on small-data tasks, supporting the hypothesis that sufficiently large pre-trained models transfer effectively.

## Conclusion

BERT demonstrates that rich, unsupervised pre-training is an integral part of many language understanding systems and that deep bidirectional architectures can generalize across a wide variety of NLP tasks with minimal task-specific adaptation. The masked language modeling objective enables bidirectional pre-training, which was previously thought to be incompatible with standard language modeling. BERT establishes a new paradigm in which a single pre-trained model is fine-tuned for diverse downstream tasks, and it underpins numerous follow-up works exploring larger models, modified objectives, and improved pre-training efficiency.
