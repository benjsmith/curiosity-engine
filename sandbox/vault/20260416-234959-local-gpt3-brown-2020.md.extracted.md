# Language Models are Few-Shot Learners

Authors: Tom B. Brown, Benjamin Mann, Nick Ryder, Melanie Subbiah, Jared Kaplan, Prafulla Dhariwal, Arvind Neelakantan, Pranav Shyam, Girish Sastry, Amanda Askell, Sandhini Agarwal, Ariel Herbert-Voss, Gretchen Krueger, Tom Henighan, Rewon Child, Aditya Ramesh, Daniel M. Ziegler, Jeffrey Wu, Clemens Winter, Christopher Hesse, Mark Chen, Eric Sigler, Mateusz Litwin, Scott Gray, Benjamin Chess, Jack Clark, Christopher Berner, Sam McCandlish, Alec Radford, Ilya Sutskever, Dario Amodei (2020)
arXiv: 2005.14165

## Abstract

Recent work has demonstrated substantial gains on many NLP tasks and benchmarks by pre-training on a large corpus of text followed by fine-tuning on a specific task. While typically task-agnostic in architecture, this method still requires task-specific fine-tuning datasets of thousands or tens of thousands of examples. By contrast, humans can generally perform a new language task from only a few examples or from simple instructions - something which current NLP systems still largely struggle to do. Here we show that scaling up language models greatly improves task-agnostic, few-shot performance, sometimes even reaching competitiveness with prior state-of-the-art fine-tuning approaches. Specifically, we train GPT-3, an autoregressive language model with 175 billion parameters, 10x more than any previous non-sparse language model, and test its performance in the few-shot setting. For all tasks, GPT-3 is applied without any gradient updates or fine-tuning, with tasks and few-shot demonstrations specified purely via text interaction with the model. GPT-3 achieves strong performance on many NLP datasets, including translation, question-answering, and cloze tasks, as well as several tasks that require on-the-fly reasoning or domain adaptation, such as unscrambling words, using a novel word in a sentence, or performing 3-digit arithmetic. At the same time, we also identify some datasets where GPT-3's few-shot learning still struggles, as well as some datasets where GPT-3 faces methodological issues related to training on large web corpora. Finally, we discuss broader societal impacts of this technology.

## Methods

GPT-3 is a family of autoregressive Transformer decoder models that predict the next token given previous tokens. The architecture follows GPT-2 closely, using modified initialization, pre-normalization, reversible tokenization, and alternating dense and locally banded sparse attention patterns similar to the Sparse Transformer. Eight model sizes are trained, ranging from 125 million parameters (GPT-3 Small) up to 175 billion parameters (GPT-3 175B). The largest model has 96 layers, hidden size 12288, 96 attention heads of dimension 128, a context window of 2048 tokens, and a batch size of 3.2M tokens at training time.

The training data is a mixture curated to balance quality and diversity. Common Crawl is filtered using a classifier trained to distinguish high-quality reference corpora from raw web pages, and fuzzy deduplication is applied to remove near-duplicates both within the dataset and against held-out benchmarks. The final mixture combines filtered Common Crawl (60%), WebText2 (22%), two Internet-based book corpora (Books1 and Books2, together 16%), and English Wikipedia (3%). Sources are upweighted during sampling based on quality rather than raw token counts, yielding a total training volume of approximately 300 billion tokens.

Training uses the Adam optimizer with beta_1 = 0.9, beta_2 = 0.95, epsilon = 1e-8, gradient clipping at global norm 1.0, a cosine learning-rate schedule with linear warmup over the first 375M tokens, and weight decay of 0.1. Learning rates are scaled by model size, following trends predicted by the Kaplan et al. scaling laws. Large models are trained with a mixture of tensor and pipeline model parallelism on clusters of V100 GPUs.

Evaluation focuses on in-context learning. In zero-shot evaluation, the model is given only a natural language description of the task. In one-shot, a single example is prepended. In few-shot, K examples (typically 10 to 100) are concatenated into the context window and the model is asked to produce a continuation that matches the demonstrated pattern. No gradient updates are performed at evaluation time. Tasks span language modeling, closed-book question answering, translation, Winograd-style tasks, common-sense reasoning, reading comprehension, SuperGLUE, natural-language inference, and synthetic reasoning benchmarks including arithmetic, word unscrambling, and SAT-style analogies.

## Key Results

GPT-3 175B sets new state-of-the-art perplexities on Penn Treebank language modeling at 20.5. On LAMBADA, it achieves 86.4% accuracy in the few-shot setting, an 18-point improvement over prior work. On TriviaQA closed-book, few-shot accuracy reaches 71.2%, surpassing fine-tuned open-domain systems. On SuperGLUE, few-shot performance approaches fine-tuned BERT-Large, with a macro average of about 71.8. Translation results are mixed but strong: GPT-3 achieves competitive BLEU scores when translating into English (for example, 39.2 on WMT Fr-En few-shot) while underperforming when translating out of English.

GPT-3 shows emergent behavior on synthetic tasks. Few-shot accuracy on 2-digit addition exceeds 99%, and on 3-digit addition reaches around 80%, rising sharply with model scale. Word-level manipulation tasks such as cycle-letter unscrambling improve from near-zero at smaller scales to 37% at 175B parameters. The paper also documents that few-shot gains over zero-shot grow substantially with scale, supporting the hypothesis that in-context learning is itself an emergent capability.

Limitations include weaker performance on some NLI datasets, difficulty with bidirectional reasoning, and sensitivity to prompt format. The authors also analyze potential data contamination between the training corpus and benchmark sets, showing that most headline results are robust to removing plausibly contaminated examples.

## Conclusion

GPT-3 shows that scaling a largely unchanged Transformer language model to 175 billion parameters yields strong few-shot performance across many language tasks without any task-specific fine-tuning. The results support the view that much of what has previously required labeled datasets can be elicited from a sufficiently large language model by prompting. The paper also highlights remaining gaps and societal risks that require continued attention.
