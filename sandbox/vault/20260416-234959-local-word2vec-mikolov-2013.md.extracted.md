### Title: Efficient Estimation of Word Representations in Vector Space

### Authors: Tomas Mikolov, Kai Chen, Greg Corrado, Jeffrey Dean (2013)

arXiv: 1301.3781

## Abstract and Introduction

Distributed representations of words as dense, low-dimensional vectors have been a subject of study in the neural network community for several decades, but producing such representations at scale from large corpora has historically been computationally prohibitive. This paper presents two new model architectures, the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBOW) model and the Continuous Skip-gram model, for learning word embeddings from unlabeled text. Both models are log-bilinear in structure and omit the nonlinear hidden layer that was standard in earlier neural language models. As a result, they can be trained on corpora with billions of tokens in a matter of hours on commodity hardware, producing vector representations of hundreds of thousands of words that exhibit remarkable semantic and syntactic structure.

The starting point of the paper is the observation that existing neural language models, such as the feed-forward neural network language model of Bengio et al. and the recurrent neural network language model of Mikolov et al., deliver high-quality word representations but are very expensive to train. The bulk of the cost comes from the projection-to-hidden-layer matrix multiplication and the final softmax over the vocabulary. By replacing the hidden layer with a simple averaging (CBOW) or removing it entirely (Skip-gram), and by using hierarchical softmax or negative sampling for the output layer, the authors obtain orders-of-magnitude training speedups with no loss of embedding quality.

A central empirical contribution of the paper is a new word analogy evaluation benchmark. The authors observe that good word vectors have the property that simple vector arithmetic corresponds to semantic or syntactic transformations: for example, vector("king") - vector("man") + vector("woman") yields a vector close to vector("queen"), and vector("Paris") - vector("France") + vector("Italy") yields a vector close to vector("Rome"). The authors assemble a dataset of roughly 20,000 such analogy questions, split into semantic and syntactic subsets, and use accuracy on this test as a proxy for embedding quality.

The paper also discusses training efficiency in detail. With the proposed architectures, the authors are able to train high-dimensional word embeddings on a 6 billion token corpus in under a day using a cluster of CPUs, whereas previous neural language models would have required weeks or months. The embeddings produced are shown to substantially outperform existing methods on the analogy task and on standard word similarity benchmarks such as WordSim-353.

The broader motivation is that distributed word representations are useful as input features to many natural language processing systems, including named entity recognition, part-of-speech tagging, machine translation, and sentiment analysis. By making high-quality embeddings easy to train on arbitrarily large corpora, the authors aim to provide a general-purpose resource for downstream NLP research. The paper explicitly emphasizes that the simplicity of the models is a feature, not a limitation: the quality of the embeddings depends primarily on the amount and quality of training data and on the dimensionality of the vectors, rather than on any particular architectural sophistication. This shifts the research focus from model design toward data and scale.

## Methods

The authors describe two model architectures, both of which learn a single embedding matrix W of shape (V, N), where V is the vocabulary size and N is the embedding dimension, and an output matrix W' of the same shape (or, in the hierarchical case, parameters of a binary tree).

**Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBOW).** Given a context window of size c around a target word w_t, the CBOW model forms a context representation by averaging the embedding vectors of the surrounding words w_{t-c}, ..., w_{t-1}, w_{t+1}, ..., w_{t+c}. This averaged vector is then used to predict w_t via a softmax over the vocabulary. Because the order of context words is ignored in the averaging, the model is effectively a bag-of-words, hence the name. The training objective is to maximize the log-likelihood of the target word given the averaged context, summed over all positions in the training corpus. Unlike earlier neural language models, there is no nonlinear hidden layer and no concatenation of context embeddings; the simple average is sufficient, and the computational cost per training example is proportional to N plus the cost of the output softmax.

**Skip-gram.** The Skip-gram model reverses the structure of CBOW: given a word w_t at the center of a window, it predicts each surrounding context word w_{t+j} for j in the window. Training maximizes the average log-probability of each context word given the center word. Skip-gram tends to produce slightly better embeddings for rare words and for semantic tasks, at the cost of requiring more training time per sentence because each center word produces multiple prediction examples.

**Computational tricks.** A naive softmax over a vocabulary of 10^5 to 10^7 words is prohibitively expensive. The authors use two approximations. The first is hierarchical softmax, in which the vocabulary is organized into a binary tree (typically a Huffman tree built from unigram frequencies) and the probability of a word is computed as the product of probabilities of traversing each edge on the path from the root to the leaf for that word. This reduces the per-example cost from O(V) to O(log V). The second is negative sampling (detailed more fully in the follow-up paper by the same authors), in which the full softmax is replaced by a logistic regression discriminating the true context word from k randomly drawn "negative" words sampled from a unigram-based noise distribution. Negative sampling is simpler to implement, is slightly faster, and in practice produces embeddings of similar or better quality, especially with k in the range of 5 to 20.

**Training setup.** The models are trained on corpora ranging from 30 million to 6 billion tokens, drawn from Google News and other sources. The authors use stochastic gradient descent with an initial learning rate that is linearly decayed to zero over training. A subsampling scheme is applied to frequent words: each token w is discarded from training with probability 1 - sqrt(t / f(w)), where f(w) is the frequency of w and t is a threshold (typically 10^-5). This improves both training speed and the quality of rare-word embeddings. Training is parallelized across CPU cores using Hogwild-style asynchronous updates, which are tolerated because each update touches only a small subset of parameters.

**Evaluation.** Embedding quality is measured on a new analogy benchmark assembled by the authors. Each question has the form "a is to b as c is to ?", and the model answers by returning the word whose embedding is closest (by cosine similarity) to vector(b) - vector(a) + vector(c), excluding a, b, and c themselves. The benchmark contains roughly 8,869 semantic analogies (capital-country, currency-country, gender relations, and so on) and 10,675 syntactic analogies (singular-plural, comparative-superlative, verb tenses, and so on). Accuracy is reported on each subset and in aggregate.

## Key Results

On the combined analogy benchmark, the Skip-gram model trained on a 6 billion token Google News corpus with 300-dimensional vectors reaches 53.3% overall accuracy, with 66.1% on the semantic subset and 65.6% on the syntactic subset. CBOW with the same dimensionality reaches 63.7% overall accuracy and is notably strong on syntactic analogies (63.0%) and semantic analogies (57.3%). For comparison, earlier feed-forward neural network language models trained with comparable settings reach only around 16% on the semantic subset.

Scaling experiments reveal a clear pattern: embedding quality improves with both corpus size and vector dimensionality, but with diminishing returns. Moving from 50-dimensional to 300-dimensional vectors on a 783 million token corpus improves overall analogy accuracy from roughly 25% to above 50%, while moving from 600-dimensional to 1000-dimensional vectors yields much smaller improvements. Similarly, training on 320 million tokens vs 6 billion tokens yields substantial gains. The authors suggest that a vector dimensionality of 300 and a training corpus of a few billion tokens represents a practical sweet spot.

On the WordSim-353 word similarity benchmark, the Skip-gram embeddings obtain Spearman correlation with human judgments that exceeds that of previous neural language model embeddings and is competitive with or superior to embeddings from distributional methods based on explicit co-occurrence counts.

Training speed is a headline result. Skip-gram with hierarchical softmax trains on 6 billion tokens in roughly a day on a single machine, whereas a comparable recurrent neural network language model would require weeks. The authors also demonstrate that learned vectors encode non-obvious regularities, such as the relationship between countries and their capital cities, even when these relationships are never explicitly supervised. A plot of PCA-projected country-capital vectors shows parallel, roughly equal-length vectors connecting each country to its capital, a striking visualization of the linear structure of the embedding space.

## Conclusion

The paper introduces two highly efficient architectures for learning distributed word representations from raw text: CBOW, which predicts a target word from an averaged context, and Skip-gram, which predicts context words from a target. Both architectures omit the expensive hidden layer of earlier neural language models and rely on hierarchical softmax or negative sampling for tractable output computation. The resulting embeddings capture rich semantic and syntactic regularities, as demonstrated on a new analogy benchmark and on standard word similarity datasets, and can be trained on corpora of billions of tokens in a day on a single machine. The authors suggest that the approach is broadly applicable and that future work should focus on using the learned vectors in downstream NLP systems, on extending the methods to phrases and longer units, and on exploring the relationship between corpus size, vector dimensionality, and embedding quality more systematically.
