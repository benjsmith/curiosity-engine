### Title: Exploring the Limits of Transfer Learning with a Unified Text-to-Text Transformer

### Authors: Colin Raffel, Noam Shazeer, Adam Roberts, Katherine Lee, Sharan Narang, Michael Matena, Yanqi Zhou, Wei Li, Peter J. Liu (Google), 2019

### arXiv: 1910.10683

## Abstract and Introduction

Transfer learning, where a model is first pre-trained on a data-rich task before being fine-tuned on a downstream task, has emerged as a powerful technique in natural language processing (NLP). The effectiveness of transfer learning has given rise to a diversity of approaches, methodologies, and practice. In this paper, we explore the landscape of transfer learning techniques for NLP by introducing a unified framework that converts all text-based language problems into a text-to-text format. Our systematic study compares pre-training objectives, architectures, unlabeled data sets, transfer approaches, and other factors on dozens of language understanding tasks. By combining the insights from our exploration with scale and our new "Colossal Clean Crawled Corpus," we achieve state-of-the-art results on many benchmarks covering summarization, question answering, text classification, and more. To facilitate future work on transfer learning for NLP, we release our dataset, pre-trained models, and code.

The basic idea underlying our work is to treat every text processing problem as a "text-to-text" problem, i.e. taking text as input and producing new text as output. This approach is inspired by previous unifying frameworks for NLP tasks, including casting all text problems as question answering (McCann et al. 2018), language modeling (Radford et al. 2019), or span extraction tasks. Crucially, the text-to-text framework allows us to directly apply the same model, objective, training procedure, and decoding process to every task we consider. We leverage this flexibility by evaluating performance on a wide variety of English-based NLP problems, including question answering, document summarization, and sentiment classification, to name a few. With this unified approach, we can compare the effectiveness of different transfer learning objectives, unlabeled data sets, and other factors, while exploring the limits of transfer learning for NLP by scaling up models and data sets beyond what has previously been considered.

We emphasize that our goal is not to propose new methods but instead to provide a comprehensive perspective on where the field stands. As such, our work primarily comprises a survey, exploration, and empirical comparison of existing techniques. We also explore the limits of current approaches by scaling up the insights from our systematic study (training models up to 11 billion parameters) to obtain state-of-the-art results in many of the tasks we consider. In order to perform experiments at this scale, we introduce the "Colossal Clean Crawled Corpus" (C4), a data set consisting of hundreds of gigabytes of clean English text scraped from the web. Recognizing that the main utility of transfer learning is the possibility of leveraging pre-trained models in data-scarce settings, we release our code, data sets, and pre-trained models.

## Methods

**Text-to-Text Framework.** Every task is cast as text-to-text. For translation: "translate English to German: That is good." -> "Das ist gut." For classification (MNLI): "mnli premise: ... hypothesis: ..." -> "entailment" / "contradiction" / "neutral". For regression (STS-B): the score is converted to a string with one-decimal granularity, e.g. "3.8". For summarization, span extraction, etc., the mapping is similarly straightforward. The same model, loss (cross-entropy over output tokens), and decoding (greedy or beam search) are used across all tasks.

**Architecture.** We use a standard encoder-decoder Transformer (Vaswani et al. 2017), with minor modifications: we remove the layer-norm bias, place layer normalization outside the residual path (pre-norm), and use a relative positional embedding scheme. Relative position embeddings are added as scalar biases to the attention logits, with bucketed buckets (32 logarithmic buckets, max offset 128). This simplifies the computation and scales to long sequences. The base architecture uses 12 layers, d_model=768, d_ff=3072, 12 attention heads, d_kv=64, giving approximately 220 million parameters. We also scale up to Small (60M), Base (220M), Large (770M), 3B, and 11B variants.

**Pre-training Objective: Span Corruption.** We compare multiple self-supervised pre-training objectives systematically, including BERT-style masked language modeling, prefix language modeling, and "deshuffling." The best-performing objective in our sweep is a span-corruption variant inspired by SpanBERT: consecutive spans of tokens are replaced with a single sentinel token, and the model learns to reconstruct the dropped spans in order, separated by sentinel tokens. We use a corruption rate of 15% and an average span length of 3 tokens. For example, the input "Thank you for inviting me to your party last week" with spans "for inviting" and "last" removed becomes "Thank you <X> me to your party <Y> week" with target "<X> for inviting <Y> last <Z>".

**Colossal Clean Crawled Corpus (C4).** We create C4 by starting from Common Crawl's April 2019 snapshot (~1.4 trillion tokens) and applying heuristic filtering: drop lines not ending in terminal punctuation, drop pages with <3 sentences, drop pages with fewer than 5 words per line on average, remove pages containing any word from a list of bad words, remove pages containing the string "javascript" or "{", remove pages containing "lorem ipsum", remove placeholder and boilerplate text, apply langdetect to filter to English (p>0.99), and deduplicate three-sentence spans. The final C4 corpus is approximately 750GB of clean English text, corresponding to about 156 billion tokens with a SentencePiece BPE vocabulary of 32000 tokens.

**Training Procedure.** Pre-training uses AdaFactor optimizer with inverse square root learning rate schedule, linear warmup over the first 10,000 steps at a base learning rate of 1.0 (with AdaFactor's parameter scaling this is effectively 0.01). For the main T5-Base model, pre-training runs for 2^19 = 524,288 steps with batch size 128 sequences of length 512 tokens, totaling approximately 34 billion tokens seen (about 1/5 of one epoch on C4). The 11B model is pre-trained for 1 million steps on batches of 2048 sequences of 512 tokens, or about 1 trillion tokens. Fine-tuning uses a constant learning rate of 0.001 with batch size 128 and sequence length 512, typically for 2^18 steps. For multi-task training and evaluation, we use task-specific prefixes and sample from each task with a temperature-scaled proportional mixing scheme.

**Systematic Comparisons.** The paper systematically varies: (1) architecture (encoder-decoder vs decoder-only vs prefix LM, parameter-shared variants); (2) pre-training objective (BERT-style, prefix LM, deshuffling, span corruption with varying span lengths and corruption rates); (3) unlabeled data (C4, C4 without filtering, RealNews, WebText, Wikipedia, Wikipedia+Books); (4) training strategy (pre-train then fine-tune, multi-task training, multi-task pre-training then fine-tuning); (5) scale (increasing training steps, batch size, model size, and ensembling).

## Key Results

The systematic study finds the encoder-decoder architecture (with parameter sharing between encoder and decoder disabled) outperforms decoder-only and prefix LM architectures at matched parameter count, by approximately 1-2 points on the GLUE average. Span-corruption objective with 15% corruption and mean span length 3 outperforms BERT-style masked language modeling by a small but consistent margin (e.g., 83.28 vs 82.87 on GLUE). C4 outperforms smaller but more curated corpora on most tasks, though Wikipedia+Books can be better on trivia-heavy QA.

On scale, increasing the number of pre-training steps by 4x and the batch size by 4x (16x more pre-training tokens) improves GLUE by about 1 point, SuperGLUE by about 2 points, and SQuAD by about 1 point. Increasing model size from Base (220M) to 11B yields substantial gains across the board.

The final T5-11B model achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on:
- GLUE average: 90.3 (prior SOTA ~89.8)
- SuperGLUE average: 89.3 (prior SOTA ~84.6; human baseline 89.8)
- SQuAD v1.1: 95.64 F1 / 90.06 EM
- CNN/Daily Mail summarization: 43.52 ROUGE-1 / 21.55 ROUGE-2 / 40.69 ROUGE-L
- WMT English-German: 32.1 BLEU
- WMT English-French: 43.4 BLEU
- WMT English-Romanian: 28.1 BLEU
- CoLA: 71.6 Matthews correlation
- MNLI-m: 92.2
- QNLI: 96.9
- SST-2: 97.5

T5 did not achieve translation SOTA because its backtranslation-free training regime is a disadvantage for low-resource translation, but it matched prior work in English-German and was competitive elsewhere.

## Conclusion

We presented a systematic empirical study of transfer learning for NLP, unified under a text-to-text framework. Our comparison of objectives, architectures, data, and transfer strategies identified span-corruption pre-training on a large, cleaned web corpus as a strong recipe. Combined with scaling to 11 billion parameters and 1 trillion pre-training tokens, the resulting T5 model achieves state-of-the-art results on GLUE, SuperGLUE, SQuAD, and CNN/Daily Mail, among other benchmarks. We release C4, all pre-trained checkpoints, and the Mesh TensorFlow training code to enable the community to build on this work. Looking forward, we expect continued returns from scale, better self-supervised objectives, and more efficient architectures, but we caution that benchmark performance has begun to saturate against human baselines on several of the tasks we consider, indicating the need for harder benchmarks.
