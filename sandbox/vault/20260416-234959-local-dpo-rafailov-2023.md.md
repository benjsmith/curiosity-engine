### Title: Direct Preference Optimization: Your Language Model is Secretly a Reward Model

### Authors: Rafael Rafailov, Archit Sharma, Eric Mitchell, Stefano Ermon, Christopher D. Manning, Chelsea Finn (2023)

### arXiv id: 2305.18290

### Abstract and Introduction

Aligning large language models to human preferences has become a central problem in deploying capable, helpful, and harmless assistants. The standard pipeline, reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), proceeds in three stages: (1) supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on instruction-following demonstrations, (2) training a reward model on pairwise human preference comparisons under a Bradley-Terry likelihood, and (3) optimizing the language model against the learned reward using policy-gradient methods such as PPO, with a KL-divergence penalty against a reference model to prevent collapse.

This pipeline has produced strong systems (InstructGPT, Anthropic's assistants, Sparrow), but stage (3) is complex, unstable, and expensive. RLHF with PPO requires sampling generations from the current policy, scoring them with the learned reward model, and running policy gradients with a KL regularizer, all in a tight online loop. Practitioners must tune KL coefficients, reward-model clipping, advantage normalization, rollout lengths, and so on. Reward hacking and catastrophic policy collapse are common failure modes.

Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) proposes a dramatic simplification. The authors derive a closed-form relationship between the reward function r and the optimal policy pi^* under the KL-constrained reward-maximization objective used in RLHF. This relationship lets them re-parameterize the reward in terms of the policy itself. Substituting into the Bradley-Terry preference likelihood yields a supervised-style loss that is optimized directly over pairwise preference data, with no explicit reward model, no sampling from the policy, and no reinforcement learning. The resulting loss is a simple binary cross-entropy on log-probability differences between the policy and a frozen reference model.

DPO matches or exceeds PPO-based RLHF on controlled sentiment generation, summarization (Reddit TL;DR), and single-turn dialogue, while being far easier to implement, much more stable, and computationally cheaper. The paper formalizes the equivalence, provides a theoretical analysis of the DPO gradient, and extensively benchmarks it against PPO, SFT, preferred-FT, and other baselines. DPO has since become the default alignment procedure for many open models (Zephyr, Tulu 2, Mixtral-Instruct) and spawned a family of related methods (IPO, KTO, ORPO, SimPO).

### Methods

Background: KL-constrained reward maximization. Given a reference policy pi_ref (typically the SFT model) and a reward function r(x, y), the RLHF objective seeks a policy pi_theta that maximizes expected reward with a KL constraint:

max_pi E_{x ~ D, y ~ pi(.|x)}[r(x, y)] - beta * KL[pi(. | x) || pi_ref(. | x)]

Under this objective, the optimal policy has a closed form:

pi^*(y | x) = (1 / Z(x)) * pi_ref(y | x) * exp(r(x, y) / beta)

where Z(x) is the partition function summing over all y. This is a Boltzmann distribution: tilt the reference by the reward scaled by 1/beta.

Re-parameterizing the reward. Solving the above for r(x, y):

r(x, y) = beta * log(pi^*(y | x) / pi_ref(y | x)) + beta * log Z(x)

The partition function Z(x) depends only on x, not y. This means any reward model r(x, y) can be written as beta * log(pi(y|x) / pi_ref(y|x)) plus an x-dependent constant, where pi is the optimal policy induced by that reward. In other words, the language model is secretly a reward model: its log-ratio against a reference implicitly defines a reward that it is simultaneously optimal for.

DPO loss. The Bradley-Terry preference model states that the probability a human prefers response y_w (winner) to y_l (loser) given prompt x is sigmoid(r(x, y_w) - r(x, y_l)). Substituting the re-parameterized reward:

r(x, y_w) - r(x, y_l) = beta * [log(pi(y_w | x) / pi_ref(y_w | x)) - log(pi(y_l | x) / pi_ref(y_l | x))]

Note that the log Z(x) terms cancel. The DPO loss is then the negative log-likelihood of the Bradley-Terry model under this re-parameterization:

L_DPO(pi) = -E_{(x, y_w, y_l) ~ D}[log sigmoid(beta * (log(pi(y_w|x) / pi_ref(y_w|x)) - log(pi(y_l|x) / pi_ref(y_l|x))))]

This loss is a simple binary cross-entropy between two log-ratio differences. Training requires only: a preference dataset of (x, y_w, y_l), the current policy pi, and a frozen reference policy pi_ref. No reward model is trained; no samples are drawn from pi; there is no RL loop. The hyperparameter beta controls the strength of the KL constraint, exactly as in standard RLHF.

Gradient analysis. The gradient of the DPO loss with respect to theta is:

grad L_DPO = -beta * E[sigmoid(r_hat(x, y_l) - r_hat(x, y_w)) * (grad log pi(y_w | x) - grad log pi(y_l | x))]

where r_hat(x, y) = beta * log(pi(y|x) / pi_ref(y|x)) is the implicit reward. The gradient increases the log-likelihood of preferred responses and decreases the log-likelihood of dispreferred ones, weighted by how incorrectly the implicit reward ranks them. When the policy already ranks y_w > y_l by a large margin, the weight becomes small, naturally implementing a margin-based learning signal. When the policy ranks them incorrectly, the weight is close to 1 and the update is aggressive.

Relationship to RLHF. Proposition 3 in the paper shows that the Bradley-Terry family of rewards has an equivalence class: two rewards that differ by an x-dependent additive constant induce the same preference distribution and the same optimal policy. DPO's re-parameterization is unique within this class, up to a gauge. Under the reward model + PPO pipeline, two stages are needed because the reward model is parameterized independently of the policy. DPO collapses both stages by recognizing that the policy itself is already a sufficient parameterization of the reward.

Training protocol. In practice, DPO training begins from an SFT checkpoint that also serves as pi_ref. A single gradient pass over the preference dataset (typically 1-3 epochs) is sufficient. Standard AdamW with small learning rates (1e-6 to 5e-7 for 7B-scale models) works well. beta is typically 0.1-0.5. Because no sampling is required, DPO training throughput is dominated by two forward/backward passes per example (one for y_w, one for y_l), plus two forward passes on the frozen reference model. Total compute is much lower than PPO, and training is stable across hyperparameters.

### Key Results

Sentiment generation (IMDB). On a controlled sentiment-generation task where ground-truth reward is known, DPO achieves higher reward-KL Pareto frontier than PPO and the "Preferred-FT" baseline across a wide range of KL budgets. At KL = 10 nats, DPO reaches reward approximately 0.9 vs PPO's 0.75; at KL = 20 nats, DPO reaches 0.95 vs PPO's 0.85. DPO dominates PPO at every KL budget tested.

Reddit TL;DR summarization (Pythia-2.8B and GPT-J). With GPT-4 as a proxy judge and comparison against human-written reference summaries, DPO achieves a 61% win rate against the reference summaries at sampling temperature 0, compared to 57% for PPO-trained RLHF, 36% for Preferred-FT, and roughly 28% for the SFT baseline. DPO dominates all baselines across temperatures 0.0 to 1.0.

Anthropic HH single-turn dialogue (Pythia-2.8B). DPO reaches a 60-64% win rate against the SFT baseline across temperatures, compared to Preferred-FT's roughly 45% and Best-of-128 sampling's 55%. PPO on this task was unstable and underperformed all baselines at matched compute.

Out-of-distribution generalization. When the SFT data distribution differs from the preference data distribution, DPO degrades gracefully; PPO shows large accuracy drops, suggesting DPO's reliance on a fixed reference policy provides some robustness.

Training stability and compute. The authors report that DPO requires no hyperparameter tuning specific to RL (no value function, no advantage estimation, no rollout schedule). On a single 8xA100 node, DPO fine-tuning of a 7B-parameter model over ~100k preference pairs completes in a few hours; equivalent PPO training requires 3-5x more wall-clock time due to policy sampling.

### Conclusion

DPO shows that the two-stage reward-model + RL pipeline of standard RLHF can be replaced by a single supervised-style objective directly on preference data. The key insight is a closed-form correspondence between reward functions and KL-constrained optimal policies, which allows the reward to be re-parameterized in terms of the policy itself. The resulting loss is a binary cross-entropy on log-probability ratios against a frozen reference model. Empirically, DPO matches or exceeds PPO-based RLHF on sentiment, summarization, and dialogue tasks, while being markedly simpler, more stable, and less compute-intensive. DPO has rapidly become a dominant alignment method for open-weights language models. Future directions include on-policy extensions that periodically refresh preferences, multi-turn and tool-use generalizations, and understanding when the implicit reward's finite expressivity (relative to an explicit parametric reward model) is a limitation.
