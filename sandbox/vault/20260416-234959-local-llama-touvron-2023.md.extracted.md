### Title: LLaMA: Open and Efficient Foundation Language Models

### Authors: Hugo Touvron, Thibaut Lavril, Gautier Izacard, Xavier Martinet, Marie-Anne Lachaux, Timothée Lacroix, Baptiste Rozière, Naman Goyal, Eric Hambro, Faisal Azhar, Aurelien Rodriguez, Armand Joulin, Edouard Grave, Guillaume Lample (2023)

### arXiv: 2302.13971

## Abstract and Introduction

We introduce LLaMA, a collection of foundation language models ranging from 7B to 65B parameters, trained exclusively on publicly available datasets. Our central finding is that it is possible to train state-of-the-art models using only publicly accessible data, without resorting to proprietary or inaccessible corpora. In particular, LLaMA-13B outperforms GPT-3 (175B) on most benchmarks despite being more than 10x smaller, and LLaMA-65B is competitive with the best models such as Chinchilla-70B and PaLM-540B. We release all our models to the research community.

The recent progress in large language models has demonstrated that scaling parameter counts can yield emergent capabilities: few-shot learning, chain-of-thought reasoning, and in-context adaptation. However, Hoffmann et al. (2022) showed with the Chinchilla work that for a given compute budget, smaller models trained on more data often outperform larger models trained on less. This shifts the optimization target from minimizing training cost to minimizing inference cost, which at deployment time dominates total cost of ownership. A smaller model trained longer is cheaper to serve at inference, which is the dominant cost at deployment scale.

Our motivation is therefore to train a series of models that achieve the best possible performance at various inference budgets, by training on more tokens than what is typically used. The resulting models, which we call LLaMA, range from 7B to 65B parameters. LLaMA-13B outperforms GPT-3 on most benchmarks despite being more than 10x smaller, and is small enough to run on a single V100 GPU, making it attractive for democratizing access to research on LLMs. At the higher end, our 65B model is competitive with the best LLMs such as Chinchilla or PaLM-540B.

Unlike Chinchilla, PaLM, or GPT-3, we only use publicly available data. Most existing models rely on data that is either not publicly available, undocumented, or requires commercial licensing. There exist some exceptions, notably OPT, GPT-NeoX, BLOOM, and GLM, but none are competitive with PaLM-62B or Chinchilla. Our work is a step toward making foundation models both capable and open.

## Methods

### Pre-training Data

Our training data is a mixture of several publicly available sources covering a diverse set of domains. We use English CommonCrawl (67%, five dumps from 2017 to 2020, deduplicated at line level, with fastText language identification and a KenLM-based quality filter removing low-quality pages), C4 (15%, the cleaned CommonCrawl corpus from Raffel et al. with deduplication and additional quality filters including presence of punctuation), GitHub (4.5%, Apache/BSD/MIT licensed projects, with heuristic filters removing boilerplate and deduplication at file level), Wikipedia (4.5%, dumps from June-August 2022 covering 20 languages), Gutenberg and Books3 (4.5%, with book-level deduplication removing near-duplicates with more than 90% content overlap), ArXiv (2.5%, LaTeX sources with removal of everything before the first section and bibliography), and Stack Exchange (2%, high-quality Q&A data across 28 topics). The entire training dataset contains approximately 1.4T tokens after tokenization. Most tokens are used only once during training, except for Wikipedia and Books domains which are repeated approximately twice.

### Tokenizer

We tokenize using byte-pair encoding (BPE) following the SentencePiece implementation. Notably, we split all numbers into individual digits and fall back to bytes to decompose unknown UTF-8 characters.

### Architecture

LLaMA is based on the transformer architecture with several modifications inspired by subsequent work:

1. **Pre-normalization (from GPT-3):** We normalize the input of each transformer sub-layer rather than the output, using RMSNorm instead of LayerNorm. RMSNorm drops the mean re-centering and uses only root mean square for stability, which is more efficient.
2. **SwiGLU activation (from PaLM):** We replace ReLU with SwiGLU, with a hidden dimension of 2/3 * 4d to keep parameter count comparable to a standard FFN with 4d hidden size.
3. **Rotary position embeddings (from GPTNeo):** We remove absolute positional embeddings and use rotary positional embeddings (RoPE) at every layer.

The different model sizes have the following configurations: LLaMA-7B uses 32 layers, 32 heads, d_model=4096, trained on 1.0T tokens. LLaMA-13B uses 40 layers, 40 heads, d_model=5120, trained on 1.0T tokens. LLaMA-33B uses 60 layers, 52 heads, d_model=6656, trained on 1.4T tokens. LLaMA-65B uses 80 layers, 64 heads, d_model=8192, trained on 1.4T tokens.

### Optimizer

We use AdamW with beta1=0.9, beta2=0.95, weight decay of 0.1, and gradient clipping at 1.0. We use a cosine learning rate schedule where the final learning rate is 10% of the peak. We apply 2000 warmup steps. Peak learning rate varies with model size: 3.0e-4 for 6.7B and 13B, and 1.5e-4 for 32.5B and 65.2B.

### Efficient Implementation

We use an efficient implementation of causal multi-head attention that avoids storing attention weights and does not compute key/query scores that are masked due to causality, based on the xformers library. This is derived from Rabe and Staats (2021) and FlashAttention (Dao et al. 2022). We also manually checkpoint activations at transformer layer boundaries to reduce memory usage. When training a 65B model on 2048 A100 GPUs with 80GB RAM, we process approximately 380 tokens per second per GPU, so training over 1.4T tokens takes about 21 days.

## Key Results

On zero-shot common sense reasoning, LLaMA-65B outperforms Chinchilla-70B on BoolQ (85.3 vs 83.7), PIQA (82.8 vs 81.8), and HellaSwag (84.2 vs 83.8). It is competitive on WinoGrande and SIQA. LLaMA-13B surpasses GPT-3 (175B) on most of these benchmarks despite being 10x smaller.

On closed-book question answering (Natural Questions, 5-shot), LLaMA-65B reaches 39.9% exact match, compared to 29.9% for GPT-3 and 31.5% for Chinchilla-70B. On TriviaQA (zero-shot), LLaMA-65B scores 68.2 vs 64.6 for Chinchilla.

On MMLU (5-shot), LLaMA-65B achieves 63.4% average accuracy compared to 67.6% for Chinchilla-70B and 69.3% for PaLM-540B. The authors note that LLaMA-65B lags here, hypothesized to be due to the limited amount of books and academic papers in the pretraining data (177GB vs 2TB for Gopher/Chinchilla).

On code generation (HumanEval, pass@1), LLaMA-65B achieves 23.7% and LLaMA-13B achieves 15.8%, comparable to PaLM-540B (26.2%) despite the significant size difference.

On mathematical reasoning (MATH, maj1@k), LLaMA-65B achieves 10.6%, lagging behind Minerva models which are specifically fine-tuned for mathematics.

Toxicity and bias evaluations on RealToxicityPrompts show that LLaMA models produce toxicity that scales with model size, similar to observations for other LLMs. CrowS-Pairs shows the model has bias preferences comparable to GPT-3 and OPT-175B.

## Conclusion

We presented LLaMA, a series of openly released language models competitive with state-of-the-art foundation models. Notably, LLaMA-13B outperforms GPT-3 while being more than 10x smaller, and LLaMA-65B is competitive with Chinchilla-70B and PaLM-540B. Unlike previous studies, we show it is possible to achieve state-of-the-art performance by training exclusively on publicly available data, without resort to proprietary datasets. We hope releasing these models to the research community will accelerate the development of LLMs, and help efforts to improve their robustness and mitigate known issues such as toxicity and bias. Additionally, we observed that fine-tuning these models on instructions leads to promising results, which we leave for future investigation.
