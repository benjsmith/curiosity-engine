### Title: LoRA: Low-Rank Adaptation of Large Language Models

### Authors: Edward J. Hu, Yelong Shen, Phillip Wallis, Zeyuan Allen-Zhu, Yuanzhi Li, Shean Wang, Lu Wang, Weizhu Chen (2021)

### arXiv id: 2106.09685

### Abstract and Introduction

As pretrained language models have scaled to hundreds of billions of parameters, full fine-tuning for each downstream task has become increasingly impractical. Storing and serving a separate copy of a 175B-parameter model like GPT-3 for every task costs roughly 350 GB per task checkpoint in fp16, and switching between tasks at inference requires expensive parameter swaps. Parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods such as adapter layers (Houlsby et al. 2019), prefix tuning (Li and Liang 2021), and prompt tuning (Lester et al. 2021) alleviate storage cost but tend to either introduce inference latency (adapters run in sequence with the frozen backbone) or reduce the effective usable context (prefix/prompt tuning consumes input tokens).

LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) proposes a simpler and more effective approach. The authors freeze the pretrained weight matrices W0 of a transformer and inject trainable rank-decomposition matrices B A into each targeted layer such that the effective weight during training becomes W0 + B A, where B is in R^{d x r}, A is in R^{r x k}, and the rank r is much smaller than min(d, k). Only A and B are trained; W0 is frozen. At inference time, B A can be merged into W0, yielding a model identical in architecture and latency to the original pretrained model. Different tasks can be swapped in and out by swapping small (B, A) pairs while sharing a single frozen backbone.

The core hypothesis, informed by prior work on the intrinsic dimensionality of pretrained models (Aghajanyan et al. 2020), is that the update to the weights during adaptation has low intrinsic rank. LoRA exploits this by explicitly constraining the update to be low-rank, which both reduces the number of trainable parameters by several orders of magnitude and acts as a regularizer.

For GPT-3 175B, LoRA reduces the number of trainable parameters by 10,000x (from 175B to roughly 17.5M at rank r = 4 applied to Q and V) and GPU memory requirements by 3x during training, while performing on par with or better than full fine-tuning on models from RoBERTa, DeBERTa, GPT-2, and GPT-3. LoRA has become the dominant approach for adapting large models and is the basis of widespread tooling including PEFT, QLoRA, and LoRA-style adapters for diffusion models.

### Methods

Problem setup. Consider adapting a pretrained autoregressive language model P_Phi(y | x) to downstream tasks. The standard approach is to initialize Phi from the pretrained weights and update Phi to Phi + Delta Phi to maximize the conditional log-likelihood on task data. Full fine-tuning requires |Delta Phi| = |Phi|. LoRA instead parameterizes Delta Phi by a much smaller set of task-specific parameters Theta with |Theta| << |Phi|.

Low-rank reparameterization. For a pretrained weight matrix W0 in R^{d x k}, LoRA constrains its update to the form Delta W = B A, where B is in R^{d x r}, A is in R^{r x k}, and the rank r satisfies r << min(d, k). During training, W0 is frozen; only A and B receive gradients. The forward pass becomes:

h = W0 x + Delta W x = W0 x + B A x

A is initialized with a random Gaussian and B is initialized to zero so that Delta W = B A = 0 at the start of training, preserving the pretrained model's behavior. The output of Delta W x is scaled by alpha / r, where alpha is a tunable constant; this decouples the effective learning rate from the rank choice. In practice the authors fix alpha and sweep only r.

Application to transformers. In a transformer, each self-attention layer contains four weight matrices: W_q, W_k, W_v, and W_o (query, key, value, output projections), plus MLP matrices. LoRA can in principle be applied to any of them. Through ablation, the authors find that applying LoRA to W_q and W_v (while leaving W_k and MLP frozen) gives the best parameter-efficiency trade-off. They typically report results with LoRA on Q and V only, which yields strong accuracy at very low r.

Number of trainable parameters. For W_q, W_v in R^{d x d}, applying rank-r LoRA adds 2 x 2 x d x r = 4 d r trainable parameters per layer (2 matrices A and B, for two weight matrices). For GPT-3 175B with d = 12288 and 96 layers, r = 4 yields roughly 4.7 M parameters for Q, V LoRA, versus 175B for full fine-tuning, a reduction of about 10,000x.

Merging at inference. Because Delta W = B A is the same shape as W0, at inference time one can compute W_eff = W0 + B A once and store only W_eff. The resulting model has the exact same architecture, memory footprint, and latency as the original pretrained model. This is in sharp contrast to adapter layers, which insert new sequential layers and add 20-30% latency per forward pass.

Training setup. The authors adapt RoBERTa-base/large and DeBERTa-XXL on GLUE; GPT-2 medium/large on E2E NLG, WebNLG, DART; and GPT-3 175B on WikiSQL, MNLI-m, and SAMSum. For each baseline (full fine-tuning, BitFit, Adapter variants, Prefix-Tuning, Prompt-Tuning), they match hyperparameter search budgets. Optimization uses AdamW with learning rate and rank sweeps. For GPT-3, they use rank r in {1, 2, 4, 8, 64} and observe that very low ranks (r = 1, 2, 4) often match or beat higher ranks.

Theoretical analysis. The paper argues that Delta W during adaptation has a low "intrinsic rank" and provides empirical support: LoRA matrices of rank 1-8 consistently recover most of the performance of full fine-tuning. They further analyze the singular value decomposition of the learned Delta W and find that Delta W amplifies task-relevant directions that already exist in W0 with small singular values, and is not simply a low-rank approximation of W0 itself. This suggests LoRA works by selectively amplifying features that are present but under-weighted in the pretrained model.

### Key Results

GLUE on RoBERTa-base (125M): full fine-tuning averages 86.4; LoRA with 0.3M trainable parameters averages 87.2, outperforming full fine-tuning while training roughly 420x fewer parameters. LoRA beats BitFit (85.4), Adapter-H (87.1), and Adapter-P (87.0) at comparable or fewer parameters.

GLUE on DeBERTa-XXL (1.5B): LoRA (4.7M trainable) averages 91.3, matching full fine-tuning (91.1) and outperforming all adapter baselines.

E2E NLG on GPT-2 medium (354M): full fine-tuning achieves BLEU 68.2; LoRA achieves 70.4 with 0.35M trainable parameters, a 0.1% fraction of the total. LoRA also improves METEOR from 46.5 to 46.8 and CIDEr from 2.47 to 2.53.

GPT-3 175B on WikiSQL: full fine-tuning reaches 73.8 validation accuracy; LoRA at rank 4 on Q, V (4.7M trainable) reaches 73.4; prefix-tuning reaches 70.1. On MNLI-m, full fine-tuning 89.5, LoRA 91.7, prefix-tuning 89.3. On SAMSum, LoRA matches full fine-tuning at roughly 4.7M trainable parameters.

Rank ablation on GPT-3: Q, V LoRA at r = 1 reaches MNLI-m 91.3; r = 2 reaches 91.5; r = 4 reaches 91.7; r = 8 reaches 91.3; r = 64 reaches 91.2. Performance is essentially flat across ranks, supporting the low intrinsic rank hypothesis.

Memory and throughput: on GPT-3 175B, LoRA reduces VRAM during training from about 1.2 TB to about 350 GB (a 3.5x savings, largely from not having to store optimizer states for frozen parameters) and improves training throughput by roughly 25% since fewer gradients are computed. Inference latency is identical to the base model after merging.

### Conclusion

LoRA is a remarkably simple parameter-efficient adaptation method: constrain the weight update Delta W to be the product of two low-rank matrices B A, keep the pretrained weights frozen, and merge B A into W0 at inference. Despite its simplicity, LoRA matches or exceeds full fine-tuning on a wide range of NLP tasks while training 10,000x fewer parameters, adding no inference latency, and enabling efficient multi-task serving. The empirical success across ranks as low as r = 1 supports the hypothesis that adaptation updates occupy a low-dimensional subspace, and that these updates amplify pretrained features rather than learning fundamentally new ones. LoRA has become the standard technique for adapting large language and diffusion models, and has inspired many extensions including AdaLoRA, QLoRA, and DoRA.
